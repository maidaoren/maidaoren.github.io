# Abstract

Why do some authoritarian regimes maintain remarkable resilience while simultaneously developing structural vulnerabilities that may undermine their long-term adaptability? Existing theories of authoritarian resilience have primarily emphasized how regimes survive through institutional adaptation, elite management, coercive capacity, and performance-based legitimacy. However, these approaches provide limited explanations for how the very mechanisms that sustain authoritarian stability can, under certain conditions, generate rigidity and increase the probability of nonlinear political transformation. This article develops an **Authoritarian Resilience-Rigidity Transformation Model (ARR Model)** to explain how digital-era authoritarian regimes may transition from adaptive stability toward increasingly rigid forms of control.

The model identifies three interconnected mechanisms underlying this transformation. First, **legitimacy production mechanisms (M1)** generate regime stability by constructing ideological coherence, performance narratives, and symbolic authority. Yet, when legitimacy becomes increasingly centralized and dependent on a narrow set of narratives, regimes may experience reduced capacity for adaptive recalibration following legitimacy shocks. Second, **social control and feedback suppression mechanisms (M2)** enhance short-term stability by limiting collective mobilization and regulating information flows. However, excessive control may weaken institutional learning by restricting accurate feedback, increasing information distortion, and reducing policy responsiveness. Third, **resource binding mechanisms (M3)** stabilize elite and social coalitions through material incentives and dependency structures, but may simultaneously constrain mobility, increase institutional rigidity, and encourage low-visibility exit strategies rather than political adaptation.

By integrating these mechanisms into a dynamic state-transition framework, this article argues that authoritarian stability is not a linear function of increasing control capacity. Instead, authoritarian systems may experience a process of **resilience-to-rigidity conversion**, in which short-term stabilization mechanisms gradually reduce adaptive capacity. The model identifies three possible governance trajectories: adaptive stability, intensified governance, and structural crisis. Transition between these states depends on the interaction among legitimacy pressures, social feedback constraints, elite coordination capacity, and accumulated institutional rigidity.

The ARR Model contributes to comparative authoritarianism literature in three ways. First, it reconceptualizes authoritarian resilience as a dynamic process containing both stabilizing and destabilizing effects. Second, it introduces a mechanism-based explanation for why digital governance technologies may simultaneously strengthen regime durability and increase long-term vulnerability. Third, it develops a nonlinear framework for understanding authoritarian transformation by identifying threshold conditions under which incremental pressures may produce abrupt systemic changes. Rather than treating authoritarian persistence and crisis as separate phenomena, this article demonstrates how they may represent different phases of the same evolutionary process.

**Keywords:** authoritarian resilience; authoritarian transformation; digital authoritarianism; political feedback; legitimacy production; state capacity; nonlinear dynamics; comparative politics.

# Introduction

## From Authoritarian Resilience to Authoritarian Rigidity: The Puzzle of Stability-Induced Vulnerability

Over the past two decades, comparative politics has witnessed a significant transformation in the study of authoritarian regimes. Contrary to earlier modernization theories that anticipated a gradual convergence toward democratization, many authoritarian governments have demonstrated remarkable durability, institutional adaptability, and capacity for survival. Rather than collapsing under economic pressures, social transformation, or technological change, numerous authoritarian regimes have developed sophisticated mechanisms of political control, elite coordination, and legitimacy construction. This empirical reality has generated a central question in contemporary comparative politics: **why do some authoritarian regimes remain resilient over extended periods, while others experience sudden episodes of institutional rigidity and nonlinear political transformation?**

Existing scholarship has provided substantial explanations for authoritarian persistence. Research on authoritarian resilience has demonstrated that non-democratic regimes are not merely dependent on coercion but often rely on complex institutional arrangements, including elite bargaining, organizational adaptation, controlled participation, and performance-based legitimacy. Institutional approaches have further shown that authoritarian survival frequently depends on the creation of formal and informal institutions that regulate succession, manage intra-elite competition, and reduce uncertainty. More recent studies of digital authoritarianism have highlighted how emerging technologies—including digital surveillance, information management systems, and algorithmic governance—have expanded the capacity of states to monitor populations, manage political communication, and prevent collective mobilization.

However, a fundamental theoretical puzzle remains unresolved. Existing approaches have primarily examined **how authoritarian regimes maintain stability**, but they provide less explanation for **how the very mechanisms that generate stability may simultaneously undermine long-term adaptability**. In other words, authoritarian resilience has largely been conceptualized as a cumulative process in which greater institutional capacity, stronger control mechanisms, and more effective legitimacy production enhance regime durability. Yet, under certain conditions, these same mechanisms may generate unintended consequences: information distortion, institutional rigidity, declining feedback capacity, excessive dependence on narrow legitimacy sources, and reduced ability to respond to emerging social pressures.

This article addresses this theoretical gap by developing an **Authoritarian Resilience-Rigidity Transformation Model (ARR Model)**. The central argument is that authoritarian stability is not a linear accumulation of control capacity. Instead, authoritarian regimes may undergo a process of **resilience-to-rigidity conversion**, in which mechanisms originally designed to enhance survival gradually reduce adaptive capacity. The model explains how short-term stabilization mechanisms can generate long-term structural vulnerabilities and why authoritarian transformation may occur through nonlinear rather than gradual processes.

# Existing Theories and Their Limitations

## Authoritarian Resilience Theory: Explaining Persistence but Not Transformation

The literature on authoritarian resilience has fundamentally reshaped the study of non-democratic regimes by challenging the assumption that authoritarian survival depends primarily on repression or institutional weakness. Scholars have demonstrated that many authoritarian regimes actively construct institutions that manage uncertainty, coordinate elites, and maintain political order.

Research on authoritarian institutions has shown that ruling elites frequently establish organizational arrangements to regulate succession, distribute resources, and constrain intra-regime conflict. Rather than lacking institutions, authoritarian regimes often rely on distinctive institutional configurations that serve different functions from those found in democratic systems. Similarly, studies of authoritarian durability have emphasized the importance of elite cohesion, opposition fragmentation, and selective incorporation as mechanisms that reduce threats to regime survival.

This scholarship has made a crucial contribution by explaining why authoritarian regimes persist. However, its primary analytical focus has been on **stability as an outcome**, rather than **stability as a dynamic process containing potential contradictions**. If authoritarian institutions are successful in reducing uncertainty, limiting opposition, and maintaining elite cohesion, a remaining question is whether these same achievements may eventually constrain adaptation.

The central limitation is therefore not that existing resilience theories are incorrect, but that they tend to treat stability-enhancing mechanisms as predominantly positive. They provide limited theoretical tools for explaining when resilience mechanisms may become sources of rigidity.

---

## Authoritarian Institutions Theory: Stability Mechanisms and the Problem of Institutional Lock-In

A second major body of research emphasizes the institutional foundations of authoritarian survival. Institutional approaches argue that authoritarian regimes create formal and informal rules that structure elite incentives, regulate political competition, and reduce uncertainty among governing actors.

This perspective has demonstrated that authoritarian institutions can produce predictable political outcomes even without democratic accountability. Power-sharing arrangements, ruling party organizations, bureaucratic structures, and elite networks can increase regime durability by providing mechanisms for coordination and conflict management.

However, institutional approaches also reveal an unresolved tension: institutions that successfully stabilize authoritarian rule may simultaneously produce institutional lock-in. As political organizations become increasingly optimized for maintaining existing arrangements, they may become less capable of processing new information, adjusting policies, or incorporating changing social demands.

This suggests that authoritarian institutions may contain a temporal paradox:

**The same mechanisms that increase short-term survival may reduce long-term adaptability.**

Existing institutional theories have not sufficiently developed a framework explaining how authoritarian systems transition from adaptive stability toward rigid equilibrium states.

---

## Digital Authoritarianism Theory: Expanding Control Capacity but Neglecting Feedback Effects

The emergence of digital technologies has introduced a new dimension into authoritarian governance. Research on digital authoritarianism has demonstrated that technological systems have expanded state capacity in areas including surveillance, information control, online censorship, and behavioral regulation.

Digital governance has altered the relationship between states and societies by enabling more precise forms of monitoring and selective intervention. Unlike twentieth-century forms of mass repression, contemporary digital systems can operate through continuous observation, data accumulation, and targeted regulation.

Nevertheless, existing digital authoritarianism research has primarily focused on the **capacity-enhancing effects** of technology. Digital tools are often analyzed as instruments that increase regime control, improve surveillance efficiency, and reduce mobilization opportunities.

This approach leaves unresolved a critical theoretical question:

**Can increasing control capacity simultaneously weaken the informational and institutional feedback systems necessary for long-term adaptation?**

A regime may successfully suppress visible opposition while becoming increasingly isolated from accurate information about social conditions. In such circumstances, digital control does not merely increase state power; it may alter the relationship between control and learning.

Therefore, digital authoritarianism requires a dynamic perspective that considers both its stabilizing and destabilizing consequences.

# The Authoritarian Resilience-Rigidity Transformation Model

This article proposes the **Authoritarian Resilience-Rigidity Transformation Model (ARR Model)** to explain how authoritarian stability mechanisms may gradually transform into sources of structural vulnerability.

The model is based on three theoretical mechanisms.

First, **legitimacy production mechanisms (M1)** explain how authoritarian regimes generate political support through ideological narratives, performance claims, historical interpretations, and symbolic authority. These mechanisms enhance stability by maintaining coherence between state narratives and social expectations. However, when legitimacy becomes increasingly concentrated around limited sources, regimes may experience declining flexibility when those sources encounter significant challenges.

Second, **social control and feedback suppression mechanisms (M2)** explain how authoritarian regimes maintain stability through surveillance, information management, and organizational fragmentation. These mechanisms reduce immediate collective action risks. Yet excessive suppression of feedback channels may reduce institutional learning capacity, increase information distortion, and weaken adaptive governance.

Third, **resource binding mechanisms (M3)** explain how regimes maintain coalitions through economic incentives, administrative dependence, and controlled access to opportunities. These mechanisms strengthen loyalty and reduce opposition incentives. However, over time, resource dependence may increase institutional rigidity by limiting autonomous actors and reducing alternative sources of adaptation.

The ARR Model therefore advances a central theoretical proposition:

> Authoritarian resilience contains an internal transformation mechanism through which stability-producing institutions may gradually generate rigidity-producing effects.

Rather than conceptualizing authoritarian trajectories as a simple continuum between stability and collapse, the model proposes a dynamic state framework consisting of three possible conditions:

1. **Adaptive Stability (S1)**
   A condition in which authoritarian institutions maintain control while preserving sufficient feedback and adjustment capacity.

2. **Intensified Governance (S2)**
   A condition characterized by increasing reliance on control mechanisms in response to perceived threats, uncertainty, or legitimacy pressures.

3. **Structural Rigidity and Crisis (S3)**
   A condition in which accumulated rigidity reduces adaptive capacity and creates conditions for nonlinear transformation.

The transition between these states is not automatic. It depends on the interaction among legitimacy pressures, social feedback constraints, elite coordination capacity, and accumulated institutional rigidity.

By conceptualizing authoritarian stability as a dynamic process rather than a fixed institutional condition, the ARR Model contributes to comparative politics in three ways.

First, it extends authoritarian resilience theory by demonstrating that resilience mechanisms may contain both stabilizing and destabilizing effects.

Second, it integrates digital governance into broader theories of authoritarian transformation by emphasizing the relationship between control capacity and feedback capacity.

Third, it provides a nonlinear framework for understanding authoritarian change by explaining why gradual accumulation of institutional pressures may produce sudden transitions rather than predictable linear decline.

The remainder of this article develops the ARR Model, derives testable hypotheses from its mechanisms, and examines its implications for comparative research on authoritarian durability and transformation.

# Theoretical Foundations: From Stability Accumulation to Stability Transformation

## 2.1 From Stability Accumulation to Stability Transformation

The central puzzle in contemporary authoritarian studies is not simply why authoritarian regimes survive, but why the mechanisms that enable survival may eventually generate conditions of reduced adaptability. Existing scholarship has produced powerful explanations for authoritarian persistence by emphasizing institutional innovation, elite coordination, organizational capacity, and technological adaptation. These approaches have demonstrated that authoritarian regimes are not inherently fragile political systems lacking institutional foundations; rather, many possess sophisticated mechanisms that allow them to manage uncertainty, suppress threats, and maintain political order over extended periods.

However, the dominant theoretical understanding of authoritarian resilience has largely conceptualized stability as an accumulative process. In this perspective, improvements in coercive capacity, institutional control, elite management, and legitimacy production generally increase regime durability. The underlying assumption is that authoritarian systems survive because they successfully solve problems of political order: they prevent elite fragmentation, reduce opposition coordination, and maintain sufficient societal compliance. Stability, therefore, is treated primarily as an outcome generated by effective governance mechanisms.

This article challenges the assumption that stability-enhancing mechanisms produce only positive effects. It argues that authoritarian resilience contains an inherent temporal tension: mechanisms that increase short-term regime stability may simultaneously reduce long-term adaptive capacity. When stabilizing mechanisms become increasingly concentrated, extensive, and self-reinforcing, they may generate unintended systemic consequences, including information distortion, institutional rigidity, declining feedback capacity, and reduced flexibility in responding to environmental change.

The theoretical shift proposed here is from a **stability accumulation model** to a **stability transformation model**.

The stability accumulation model assumes:

[
\text{Greater Control Capacity} \rightarrow \text{Greater Stability}
]

The stability transformation model proposed in this article argues:

[
\text{Greater Control Capacity}
\rightarrow
\text{Short-term Stability}
\rightarrow
\text{Reduced Adaptability}
\rightarrow
\text{Potential Long-term Rigidity}
]

The key theoretical claim is not that authoritarian institutions necessarily produce instability, nor that stronger control mechanisms inevitably undermine regimes. Rather, the argument is conditional: **under specific institutional and informational conditions, mechanisms designed to preserve stability may gradually transform into mechanisms that constrain adaptation.**

This process is defined as the **Stability Transformation Effect**.

# The Stability Transformation Effect

The concept of the **Stability Transformation Effect** refers to the process through which authoritarian stability mechanisms undergo a functional transformation over time: from instruments that enhance regime resilience into structures that generate institutional rigidity.

The concept rests on three theoretical observations.

First, political institutions operate across multiple temporal horizons. A mechanism that produces beneficial outcomes in the short term may generate different consequences when accumulated over longer periods. For example, extensive information control may reduce immediate political uncertainty by limiting disruptive communication. However, when information filtering becomes excessive, it may also reduce the accuracy of signals received by governing institutions, weakening their ability to identify emerging problems.

Second, authoritarian systems are adaptive systems rather than static structures. Their survival depends not only on their capacity to impose control but also on their capacity to process information, adjust policies, and reorganize institutional arrangements. Therefore, any mechanism that increases control while simultaneously decreasing adaptive feedback may create a trade-off between stability and flexibility.

Third, authoritarian stability mechanisms are embedded within self-reinforcing processes. Once political systems become increasingly dependent on particular forms of legitimacy, organizational structures, or resource distribution networks, the cost of adjustment may increase. Over time, the mechanisms that once enabled adaptation may become sources of institutional lock-in.

Thus, the Stability Transformation Effect does not imply a simple decline from stability to collapse. Instead, it describes a dynamic process in which authoritarian regimes may move from:

\text{Adaptive Resilience}
]

\text{Rigid Stability}

\text{Structural Transformation}

# 2.2 Authoritarian Resilience and the Logic of Stability Accumulation

The literature on authoritarian resilience has fundamentally transformed comparative politics by demonstrating that authoritarian survival cannot be explained solely through repression. Earlier theories often associated authoritarian regimes with institutional weakness, personal rule, and limited capacity for adaptation. In contrast, contemporary scholarship has shown that many authoritarian regimes develop sophisticated mechanisms of institutional management.

The resilience perspective emphasizes several core mechanisms.

First, authoritarian regimes maintain stability through **elite coordination mechanisms**. By regulating intra-elite competition, distributing political opportunities, and managing succession processes, regimes reduce uncertainty among ruling coalitions. Stability emerges because political actors recognize institutional constraints and incentives.

Second, authoritarian regimes employ **organizational adaptation mechanisms**. Ruling parties, bureaucratic organizations, and administrative institutions allow regimes to penetrate society, collect information, and implement policies.

Third, authoritarian regimes construct **legitimacy mechanisms** through economic performance, ideological narratives, nationalism, or institutional achievements. These mechanisms reduce reliance on coercion alone by producing forms of acceptance, compliance, or political accommodation.

Within this theoretical tradition, authoritarian resilience is generally understood as a capacity-building process. The stronger the institutions, the greater the regime's ability to withstand internal and external pressures.

This perspective has generated significant analytical advances. However, it also leaves an unresolved theoretical question:

**Can the accumulation of resilience mechanisms eventually reduce resilience itself?**

If authoritarian systems continuously strengthen mechanisms of control, coordination, and legitimacy production, they may become increasingly effective at maintaining existing arrangements. Yet the same effectiveness may reduce their ability to modify those arrangements when circumstances change.

The problem is therefore not whether authoritarian resilience exists, but whether resilience contains internal limits.

The Stability Transformation Effect addresses this question by conceptualizing resilience as a dynamic process rather than a permanent institutional advantage.

# Stability Mechanisms as Dual-Effect Institutions

The Stability Transformation Effect builds upon a broader institutional insight: political mechanisms often generate multiple effects across different time horizons.

Institutions are not simply functional instruments designed to achieve a single objective. Their effects depend on how they interact with information flows, incentives, organizational structures, and environmental conditions.

Within authoritarian systems, mechanisms of stability generally perform an immediate stabilizing function.

For example:

* stronger surveillance reduces uncertainty regarding opposition activity;
* centralized legitimacy narratives increase ideological coherence;
* resource distribution strengthens elite and social dependence.

However, these same mechanisms may generate secondary consequences.

A highly centralized information system may improve monitoring capacity while reducing the diversity of information available to decision-makers.

A highly concentrated legitimacy structure may strengthen ideological consistency while reducing narrative flexibility.

A highly dependent resource allocation system may strengthen loyalty while weakening autonomous adaptation.

Therefore, the relationship between stability mechanisms and regime durability is not purely additive. Instead, it is conditional and dynamic.

The same mechanism may operate differently depending on its intensity, duration, and institutional context.

> The effectiveness of authoritarian stability mechanisms depends not only on their capacity to suppress immediate threats but also on their impact on long-term adaptive capacity.

This assumption differentiates the ARR Model from traditional resilience theories by introducing a temporal dimension. Stability mechanisms are not inherently stabilizing or destabilizing; rather, their effects change depending on institutional intensity, duration, and systemic interaction.

The second theoretical implication is:

> Authoritarian resilience may contain the conditions of authoritarian rigidity when stabilization mechanisms reduce the system’s ability to process information and adjust.

---

# Assumption 3: Nonlinear Transition Assumption

## Authoritarian Transformation as a Threshold Process

The third assumption concerns the dynamics of authoritarian transformation.

Many approaches to political change implicitly assume a relatively linear relationship between accumulated pressures and regime outcomes. Under such a framework, increasing economic difficulties, social dissatisfaction, or institutional weaknesses should gradually increase the probability of political transformation.

The ARR Model rejects this linear assumption.

Instead, it argues that authoritarian systems often operate through threshold dynamics:

[
\text{Accumulated Pressure}
\neq
\text{Immediate Transformation}
]

Rather:

[
\text{Accumulated Pressure}
+
\text{Reduced Adaptive Capacity}

>

\text{Critical Threshold}
]

which produces:

[
\text{Nonlinear System Transition}
]

In other words, authoritarian regimes may absorb significant levels of pressure while remaining stable because existing mechanisms continue to function. However, once accumulated rigidity exceeds the system’s ability to adjust, relatively small additional shocks may produce disproportionate consequences.

This process resembles a state transition rather than gradual decline.

The model identifies several potential threshold conditions:

### 1. Legitimacy Threshold

When existing legitimacy mechanisms become insufficient to reconcile social expectations with political narratives.

### 2. Feedback Threshold

When information distortion becomes sufficiently severe that institutions lose the ability to accurately perceive social conditions.

### 3. Coordination Threshold

When elite or bureaucratic coordination mechanisms become unable to manage competing interests.

### 4. Adaptation Threshold

When institutional adjustment becomes more costly than maintaining existing structures.

Therefore, authoritarian transformation should not be understood as a simple accumulation of dissatisfaction or pressure. Instead, transformation occurs when pressure interacts with declining adaptive capacity.

This assumption generates the third theoretical implication:

> Authoritarian systems may remain stable despite accumulating pressures until critical thresholds are reached, after which political change may occur rapidly and disproportionately.

---

# Integrating the Three Assumptions: The Logic of the ARR Model

The three assumptions together establish the theoretical foundation of the Authoritarian Resilience-Rigidity Transformation Model.

The model begins with the Adaptive Stability Assumption:

[
Stability=f(Legitimacy,Control,Resources)
]

which explains how authoritarian regimes maintain equilibrium.

The Dual Effect Assumption then introduces the possibility that these mechanisms generate unintended rigidity:

[
Stability\ Mechanisms
\rightarrow
Short-term\ Resilience
+
Long-term\ Rigidity
]

Finally, the Nonlinear Transition Assumption explains how rigidity accumulation may eventually produce systemic transformation:

[
Accumulated\ Rigidity

>

Adaptive\ Capacity
\rightarrow
State\ Transition
]

Together, these assumptions produce the central theoretical proposition of the ARR Model:

> **Authoritarian regimes are not only maintained by mechanisms of stability; they are also transformed by the long-term consequences of those same mechanisms. When stability-producing institutions increasingly constrain adaptation, authoritarian resilience may gradually convert into authoritarian rigidity, creating the conditions for nonlinear political transformation.**

The next section develops this theoretical argument by examining the three causal mechanisms through which resilience is transformed into rigidity: legitimacy production, social control and feedback suppression, and resource binding.

# 2.3 Three Mechanisms of Resilience-Rigidity Transformation

## From Stability Production to Rigidity Accumulation

The Authoritarian Resilience-Rigidity Transformation Model (ARR Model) identifies three interconnected mechanisms through which authoritarian stability may gradually transform into institutional rigidity:

1. **Legitimacy Production Mechanism (M1)**
2. **Social Control and Feedback Suppression Mechanism (M2)**
3. **Resource Binding Mechanism (M3)**

Existing theories of authoritarian durability have demonstrated that these mechanisms are central sources of regime stability. However, the ARR Model introduces a temporal and dynamic perspective by arguing that these mechanisms are not permanently stability-enhancing. Instead, their effects may change as institutional intensity increases and adaptive capacity declines.

The core proposition is:

> Stability mechanisms do not disappear when authoritarian systems become rigid; rather, they continue operating while their functional consequences gradually transform from resilience-enhancing mechanisms into rigidity-producing mechanisms.

Therefore, the key analytical question is not whether authoritarian regimes possess legitimacy, control, or resource distribution mechanisms. The question is:

**Under what conditions do these mechanisms shift from supporting adaptation to constraining adaptation?**

The following sections examine the three causal pathways through which this transformation occurs.

---

# M1: Legitimacy Production Mechanism

## From Adaptive Legitimacy to Rigid Legitimacy

## 1. Existing Theory Explanation

A large body of authoritarian research has emphasized legitimacy as a central component of regime durability. Earlier approaches often associated authoritarian survival primarily with coercive capacity, but subsequent scholarship demonstrated that authoritarian regimes frequently seek forms of legitimacy beyond repression.

Legitimacy production allows regimes to establish normative justification, political acceptance, and social compliance. It may derive from multiple sources, including:

* economic performance;
* institutional effectiveness;
* historical narratives;
* ideological commitments;
* national identity construction.

From this perspective, legitimacy functions as a stabilizing mechanism because it reduces reliance on coercion and creates broader forms of political accommodation.

The traditional theoretical relationship can be represented as:

[
\text{Legitimacy Production}
\rightarrow
\text{Political Acceptance}
\rightarrow
\text{Regime Stability}
]

However, the ARR Model argues that legitimacy production is not a static resource. It evolves through two potential stages:

[
\text{Adaptive Legitimacy}
\rightarrow
\text{Rigid Legitimacy}
]

---

# 2. Mechanism Operation

In the initial stage, authoritarian regimes often maintain legitimacy through multiple overlapping sources.

A diversified legitimacy structure allows regimes to adapt narratives according to changing conditions.

For example:

* economic performance may support output legitimacy;
* institutional effectiveness may support administrative legitimacy;
* historical narratives may support symbolic legitimacy;
* ideological narratives may provide broader political meaning.

This multidimensional legitimacy structure creates flexibility because failure in one dimension does not necessarily undermine the entire legitimacy system.

The mechanism can be expressed as:

[
\text{Legitimacy Diversity}
\rightarrow
\text{Narrative Flexibility}
\rightarrow
\text{Adaptive Capacity}
]

However, over time, legitimacy systems may become increasingly concentrated around a narrower set of narratives, institutions, or symbolic sources.

When legitimacy becomes dependent on a highly centralized narrative structure, the system enters a condition of:

## Narrative Lock-in

（叙事锁定）

Narrative lock-in refers to a situation in which political narratives become increasingly difficult to revise because institutional credibility becomes tied to maintaining consistency rather than responding to changing conditions.

The mechanism becomes:

[
\text{Legitimacy Concentration}
\uparrow
]

↓

[
\text{Narrative Flexibility}
\downarrow
]

↓

[
\text{Adaptation Capacity}
\downarrow
]

---

# 3. Short-Term Stabilizing Effect

In the short term, legitimacy concentration may strengthen authoritarian stability.

A coherent and centralized legitimacy narrative can:

* reduce ideological uncertainty;
* strengthen elite coordination;
* simplify political communication;
* reinforce shared expectations.

When political actors receive consistent signals regarding institutional goals and political identity, coordination costs decrease.

Therefore:

[
\text{Narrative Consistency}
\rightarrow
\text{Elite Cohesion}
\rightarrow
\text{Short-term Stability}
]

This explains why concentrated legitimacy structures may initially enhance regime resilience.

---

# 4. Long-Term Rigidity Effect

The same mechanism may generate rigidity when adaptation requires narrative adjustment.

As legitimacy becomes increasingly dependent on a limited number of sources, revising those narratives may become politically costly.

The regime faces a dilemma:

* maintaining narrative consistency preserves short-term credibility;
* modifying narratives may improve long-term adaptation.

When narrative adjustment becomes difficult, legitimacy production changes from an adaptive mechanism into a constraint.

The long-term pathway becomes:

[
\text{Legitimacy Concentration}
\rightarrow
\text{Narrative Lock-in}
\rightarrow
\text{Reduced Policy Flexibility}
\rightarrow
\text{Institutional Rigidity}
]

Thus, the problem is not a lack of legitimacy production, but excessive dependence on a rigid legitimacy structure.

---

# 5. Observable Implications

The ARR Model predicts that legitimacy rigidity should be observable through several indicators:

1. Increasing concentration of official legitimacy narratives.
2. Reduced diversity of institutional justification.
3. Higher political costs associated with narrative adjustment.
4. Greater divergence between official narratives and changing social expectations.

The key prediction is:

> The relationship between legitimacy concentration and regime stability is not linear. Moderate levels of legitimacy coherence may enhance resilience, while excessive concentration may reduce adaptive capacity.

---

# M2: Social Control and Feedback Suppression Mechanism

## From Control Enhancement to Feedback Deficit

## 1. Existing Theory Explanation

Social control has traditionally been viewed as one of the most important mechanisms of authoritarian durability.

Authoritarian regimes maintain stability by regulating:

* collective organization;
* information circulation;
* political mobilization;
* opposition coordination.

Existing theories generally emphasize the stabilizing function of control:

[
\text{Control Capacity}
\uparrow
\rightarrow
\text{Collective Action Difficulty}
\downarrow
\rightarrow
\text{Regime Stability}
\uparrow
]

Digital technologies have further expanded this capacity by allowing more continuous monitoring, information management, and targeted intervention.

From this perspective, stronger control mechanisms increase regime resilience.

However, the ARR Model introduces a critical distinction between **control capacity** and **feedback capacity**.

A system may become increasingly capable of controlling society while simultaneously becoming less capable of understanding society.

---

# 2. Mechanism Operation

The central mechanism is the:

## Feedback Suppression Paradox

Control mechanisms operate through two simultaneous pathways.

The first pathway is stabilizing:

[
\text{Control Intensity}
\uparrow
]

↓

[
\text{Collective Resistance}
\downarrow
]

↓

[
\text{Immediate Stability}
\uparrow
]

However, the second pathway operates in the opposite direction:

[
\text{Control Intensity}
\uparrow
]

↓

[
\text{Information Accuracy}
\downarrow
]

↓

[
\text{Policy Learning}
\downarrow
]

This creates a condition defined as:

## Feedback Deficit

（反馈赤字）

Feedback deficit refers to a situation where governing institutions retain strong capacity to control social behavior but lose capacity to accurately perceive social conditions.

---

# 3. Short-Term Stabilizing Effect

In the short term, intensified control mechanisms provide several benefits:

* reduce coordination among challengers;
* increase predictability of political environments;
* prevent rapid mobilization;
* strengthen administrative control.

Therefore, control mechanisms contribute directly to regime survival.

The short-term effect is:

[
\text{Control}
\rightarrow
\text{Order Maintenance}
]

---

# 4. Long-Term Rigidity Effect

Over time, excessive control may weaken the information-processing function of political systems.

When individuals and institutions anticipate political costs associated with negative information, they may reduce accurate reporting or avoid expressing concerns.

This creates:

* distorted information flows;
* reduced policy correction;
* declining institutional learning.

The long-term pathway becomes:

[
\text{Control Intensification}
\rightarrow
\text{Feedback Suppression}
\rightarrow
\text{Information Distortion}
\rightarrow
\text{Adaptive Decline}
]

Therefore, a highly controlled system may become increasingly stable in appearance while becoming less capable of adaptation.

---

# 5. Observable Implications

The model predicts that high-control environments may exhibit:

1. Increasing gap between official information and social conditions.
2. Reduced policy responsiveness.
3. Lower diversity of institutional feedback channels.
4. Greater dependence on centralized information sources.

The theoretical prediction is:

> Control capacity may enhance short-term stability while simultaneously reducing long-term adaptive capacity through feedback suppression.

---

# M3: Resource Binding Mechanism

## 1. Existing Theory Explanation

Resource distribution has long been recognized as an important mechanism of authoritarian survival.

Authoritarian regimes maintain coalitions through:

* economic benefits;
* institutional opportunities;
* professional advancement;
* access to state-controlled resources.

This perspective emphasizes exchange relationships:

[
\text{Resource Allocation}
\rightarrow
\text{Political Loyalty}
\rightarrow
\text{Regime Stability}
]

Resource binding reduces incentives for opposition by increasing the benefits associated with cooperation.

---

# 2. Mechanism Operation

The ARR Model extends this perspective by emphasizing the structural consequences of long-term dependency.

Resource binding operates through a dependency mechanism:

[
\text{Resource Dependency}
\uparrow
]

↓

[
\text{Open Resistance}
\downarrow

However, reduced resistance does not necessarily indicate increased support.

]

This mechanism is conceptualized as:

## Exit without Voice

The concept extends Hirschman's classic framework by suggesting that under high dependency conditions, individuals may avoid direct political expression while seeking private forms of withdrawal.

Examples include:

* reduced public engagement;
* social disengagement;
* migration preferences;
* economic withdrawal;
* private adaptation strategies.

---

# 3. Short-Term Stabilizing Effect

Resource binding strengthens authoritarian stability by:

* maintaining elite coalitions;
* reducing incentives for confrontation;
* increasing dependence on existing institutions.

The short-term pathway is:

[
\text{Resource Binding}
\rightarrow
\text{Coalitional Stability}
\rightarrow

]

---

# 4. Long-Term Rigidity Effect

However, excessive resource dependency may weaken social autonomy and institutional flexibility.

When access to resources becomes strongly tied to existing structures, actors may prioritize individual adaptation over collective problem-solving.

This creates a paradox:

Visible opposition decreases, but underlying social disengagement increases.

The long-term pathway becomes:

---

# 5. Observable Implications

The model predicts that highly resource-dependent systems may demonstrate:

1. Low levels of public political participation.
2. Increasing private withdrawal strategies.
3. Declining social trust.
4. Reduced autonomous organizational capacity.

The theoretical prediction is:

> Resource binding can stabilize political coalitions while simultaneously weakening broader social adaptability.

---

# Integrating the Three Mechanisms

The three mechanisms together constitute the causal foundation of the ARR Model.

They demonstrate three pathways through which authoritarian resilience may transform into rigidity:

| Mechanism             | Short-term Function | Long-term Risk              |

The central theoretical implication is:

> **Authoritarian systems do not necessarily lose resilience because their stabilizing mechanisms collapse. Rather, resilience may transform into rigidity when those mechanisms become so deeply embedded that they preserve existing structures at the expense of adaptive capacity.**

# 2.4 Dynamic State Transition Model

## From Adaptive Stability to Structural Rigidity: A Nonlinear Theory of Authoritarian Transformation

The previous sections established the theoretical foundations of the Authoritarian Resilience-Rigidity Transformation Model (ARR Model) by demonstrating how authoritarian stability mechanisms may generate both resilience and rigidity. However, identifying individual mechanisms is insufficient to explain how authoritarian systems evolve over time.

A central theoretical challenge in comparative authoritarian studies is explaining why some regimes remain stable despite significant pressures, while others experience rapid institutional transformation. If authoritarian systems possess similar mechanisms of legitimacy production, social control, and resource distribution, why do their trajectories diverge?

The ARR Model addresses this question by introducing a **dynamic state transition framework**.

Unlike approaches that conceptualize authoritarian change as a gradual decline from stability to collapse, the ARR Model argues that authoritarian systems operate through different equilibrium states. Political transformation occurs when accumulated institutional pressures exceed the system’s adaptive capacity, producing a transition between states.

The model identifies three primary states:

1. **Adaptive Authoritarian Stability (S1)**
2. **Intensified Governance (S2)**
3. **Structural Rigidity Crisis (S3)**

The basic transition logic is:

[
S1 \rightarrow S2 \rightarrow S3
]

However, this process is neither inevitable nor linear.

The central theoretical argument is:

> Intensified governance does not necessarily produce authoritarian crisis. The outcome depends on whether intensified control preserves adaptive capacity or accelerates rigidity accumulation.

Therefore, S2 represents a critical intermediate state in which authoritarian systems may either recover, stabilize, or move toward structural rigidity.

---

# 2.4.1 State 1: Adaptive Authoritarian Stability (S1)

## Stability with Feedback and Adjustment Capacity

The first state of the ARR Model is **Adaptive Authoritarian Stability (S1)**.

This state represents an authoritarian equilibrium in which governing institutions successfully maintain political order while preserving sufficient capacity for learning and adjustment.

The defining characteristic of S1 is not the absence of control.

Rather, it is the coexistence of:

* effective control mechanisms;
* functioning feedback channels;
* institutional adjustment capacity.

The relationship can be represented as:

[
Control Capacity
+
Feedback Capacity
+
Adjustment Capacity
===================

Adaptive Stability
]

In this state, authoritarian institutions are capable of managing threats without completely suppressing information flows.

Three characteristics define S1.

---

## First, controlled openness of information systems

The regime maintains control over political competition but still retains mechanisms for collecting information about social conditions.

Information flows remain sufficiently diverse to support policy adjustment.

Therefore:

[
Control
\neq
Information Isolation
]

Control mechanisms regulate political challenges but do not eliminate all feedback.

---

## Second, flexible legitimacy production

Legitimacy is generated through multiple sources rather than a single rigid narrative.

The regime can adjust emphasis among:

* performance;
* institutional effectiveness;
* ideological narratives;
* national identity.

This creates legitimacy resilience.

The system can absorb limited shocks because failure in one legitimacy dimension does not necessarily undermine the entire structure.

---

## Third, adaptive elite coordination

Elite institutions retain mechanisms for negotiation, adjustment, and conflict management.

Political stability derives not only from elite unity but from the capacity to manage internal differences.

Therefore, S1 represents a condition of:

[
High Stability
+
High Adaptability
]

---

# Transition from S1 to S2

The movement from S1 toward intensified governance occurs when the system encounters significant internal or external pressures.

Potential triggers include:

* legitimacy pressure;
* economic uncertainty;
* social mobilization;
* elite competition;
* external security challenges.

However, the critical factor is not pressure itself.

The key variable is:

[
Pressure
\times
Adaptive Capacity
]

If adaptive capacity remains strong, pressure may be absorbed.

If adaptive capacity declines, regimes may increasingly rely on intensified control mechanisms.

This produces the transition:

[
S1
\rightarrow
S2
]

---

# 2.4.2 State 2: Intensified Governance (S2)

## The Critical Transformation Zone

The second state of the ARR Model is **Intensified Governance (S2)**.

S2 represents a condition in which authoritarian regimes increase reliance on stabilization mechanisms in response to perceived threats or uncertainty.

This state is analytically important because it is not equivalent to crisis.

S2 may produce two different outcomes:

[
S2
\rightarrow
Recovery
]

or:

[
S2
\rightarrow
S3
]

The difference depends on whether intensified governance preserves institutional adaptability or accelerates rigidity.

The model identifies three forms of intensified governance:

1. Defensive Intensification (S2-D)
2. Offensive Intensification (S2-O)
3. Vision-driven Intensification (S2-V)

---

# S2-D: Defensive Intensification

## Response to External or Internal Pressure

Defensive intensification occurs when regimes increase control mechanisms primarily as a response to perceived threats.

Typical triggers include:

* external security pressure;
* domestic instability;
* economic uncertainty;
* elite conflicts.

The logic is:

[
Threat Perception
\uparrow
\rightarrow
Control Intensity
\uparrow
]

The key characteristic of S2-D is that intensified governance remains primarily reactive.

If institutions preserve feedback capacity and maintain policy flexibility, S2-D may return to S1.

The pathway is:

[
S2-D
\rightarrow
S1
]

However, if defensive measures become permanent and institutionalized, they may produce rigidity:

[
S2-D
\rightarrow
S3
]

The difference depends on whether emergency mechanisms become normalized governance structures.

---

# S2-O: Offensive Intensification

## Expansion of Control Beyond Threat Response

Offensive intensification differs from defensive intensification because increased control is not merely reactive.

Instead, the regime actively expands intervention capacity.

The logic becomes:

[
Control Capacity
\uparrow
\rightarrow
Control Scope
\uparrow
]

This may involve expanding administrative penetration, increasing regulation, or extending political oversight into previously autonomous areas.

In the short term, S2-O may strengthen regime coherence.

However, the risk is that expanding control increases:

* institutional complexity;
* information filtering;
* administrative burden.

The transformation pathway becomes:

[
Control Expansion
\rightarrow
Feedback Reduction
\rightarrow
Institutional Rigidity
]

Therefore, S2-O has a higher probability of moving toward S3 when adjustment mechanisms are weak.

---

# S2-V: Vision-driven Intensification

## Governance Through Transformative Objectives

Vision-driven intensification occurs when political systems increase control not only because of threats but because of long-term transformative goals.

The defining feature is the pursuit of a comprehensive political, ideological, or developmental vision.

The mechanism is:

[
Political Vision
\rightarrow
Institutional Mobilization
\rightarrow
Governance Intensification
]

Unlike S2-D, which responds to threats, S2-V is driven by strategic objectives.

Its outcome depends heavily on institutional flexibility.

If institutions can revise strategies based on feedback, S2-V may produce adaptation.

If political objectives become resistant to correction, S2-V may generate rigidity.

The critical variable is:

[
Vision Commitment
vs.
Institutional Learning Capacity
]

---

# 2.4.3 State 3: Structural Rigidity Crisis (S3)

## When Stability Mechanisms Become Constraints

The third state is **Structural Rigidity Crisis (S3)**.

Importantly, S3 does not necessarily mean immediate regime collapse.

Instead, it represents a condition in which authoritarian systems lose adaptive capacity while maintaining significant control capability.

The defining feature of S3 is the divergence between:

[
Control Capacity
]

and:

[
Adaptation Capacity
]

The regime may remain powerful in terms of surveillance, coercion, and organizational control, while becoming increasingly limited in its ability to adjust.

Three characteristics define S3.

---

# First: Declining Adjustment Capacity

Institutions become increasingly optimized for maintaining existing arrangements rather than responding to new conditions.

Policy change becomes more difficult because adaptation threatens established interests and narratives.

---

# Second: Elite Coordination Problems

As institutional rigidity increases, elite coordination mechanisms may become less flexible.

The problem is not necessarily elite conflict itself.

Rather:

[
Coordination Mechanisms
\downarrow
]

because institutional adjustment becomes increasingly costly.

---

# Third: Information Distortion

Feedback systems become weaker as actors have greater incentives to provide information consistent with institutional expectations.

The result is:

[
Information Quantity
\neq
Information Quality
]

A system may possess large amounts of data while losing the ability to interpret social realities accurately.

---

# 2.4.4 Outcomes of Structural Rigidity: Institutional Freeze and Political Crisis

S3 does not produce a single outcome.

The ARR Model identifies two possible trajectories.

---

## Path 1: Institutional Freeze

[
S3
\rightarrow
Institutional\ Freeze
]

Institutional freeze occurs when a regime maintains control but loses transformative capacity.

Characteristics include:

* persistent stability;
* declining innovation;
* reduced institutional flexibility;
* prolonged stagnation.

The system does not collapse but enters a low-adaptation equilibrium.

---

## Path 2: Political Crisis

[
S3
\rightarrow
Political\ Crisis
]

Political crisis occurs when accumulated rigidity interacts with significant shocks.

Potential triggers include:

* severe legitimacy disruption;
* elite fragmentation;
* economic shocks;
* external crises.

Because adaptive capacity has already declined, relatively limited shocks may generate disproportionate effects.

This produces nonlinear transformation.

---

# 2.4.5 The Nonlinear Logic of State Transition

The ARR Model differs from linear theories of authoritarian decline by emphasizing threshold effects.

The relationship is not:

[
Pressure
\uparrow
========

Crisis
\uparrow
]

Instead:

[
Pressure
+
Rigidity

>

Adaptive\ Capacity
]

produces:

[

]

The complete dynamic model can be summarized as:

```
                 Internal / External Shock

                         ↓


        S1 Adaptive Authoritarian Stability

                         ↓

        S2 Intensified Governance

          ↙             ↓             ↘

       S2-D           S2-O          S2-V

                         ↓

        S3 Structural Rigidity Crisis


              ↙                    ↘

   Institutional Freeze       Political Crisis

```

---

# Theoretical Implications

The Dynamic State Transition Model generates three major theoretical contributions.

First, it explains why authoritarian regimes with similar control capacities may follow different trajectories. The decisive factor is not control intensity alone but the relationship between control and adaptive capacity.

Second, it explains why intensified governance is a critical analytical stage rather than simply a sign of authoritarian decline. S2 represents a branching point where regimes may either restore equilibrium or accumulate rigidity.

Third, it provides a nonlinear framework for understanding authoritarian transformation. Political change may appear sudden not because pressures emerge suddenly, but because accumulated rigidity remains hidden until the system crosses a critical threshold.

The central proposition of the dynamic model is:

> **Authoritarian systems do not transition from stability to crisis because they lose control; they transition when the mechanisms that preserve control increasingly limit their ability to adapt.**

# 2.5 Theory Summary: The Authoritarian Resilience-Rigidity Transformation Model (ARR Model)

## 2.5 理论总结：威权韧性—刚性转化模型

The preceding sections have established that authoritarian stability cannot be understood solely as a function of increasing control capacity. Existing approaches have demonstrated how authoritarian regimes maintain order through institutional arrangements, elite coordination, legitimacy construction, and coercive capabilities. However, these mechanisms may generate different consequences over time depending on whether they preserve or undermine the regime’s adaptive capacity.

The **Authoritarian Resilience-Rigidity Transformation Model (ARR Model)** integrates these mechanisms into a dynamic framework. The central proposition of the model is that authoritarian resilience is not a fixed institutional property, but an evolving relationship between **stabilization capacity** and **adaptive capacity**.

The model argues that authoritarian regimes rely on three fundamental stability-producing mechanisms:

1. **Legitimacy Production Mechanism (M1)**
2. **Social Control and Feedback Management Mechanism (M2)**
3. **Resource Binding Mechanism (M3)**

Together, these mechanisms generate authoritarian stability by reducing uncertainty, coordinating political actors, managing social pressures, and maintaining compliance.

However, the ARR Model introduces a critical theoretical distinction:

> The same mechanisms that enhance authoritarian resilience in the short term may contribute to authoritarian rigidity when they reduce institutional adaptability over time.

This transformation occurs through three potential processes:

* **Legitimacy Lock-in**: when legitimacy becomes increasingly dependent on concentrated narratives or limited sources of justification, reducing ideological flexibility.
* **Feedback Suppression**: when enhanced control mechanisms restrict information flows and weaken institutional learning capacity.
* **Resource Dependency**: when resource-based political integration creates excessive dependence and limits autonomous adaptation.

Therefore, authoritarian stability is not conceptualized as a linear accumulation of governing capacity. Instead, the ARR Model proposes a dynamic relationship between resilience and rigidity.

## The Dynamic Logic of the ARR Model

The ARR Model identifies three possible trajectories of authoritarian development.

---

## 1. Adaptive Stability (S1): Resilience without Rigidity

In the first trajectory, authoritarian regimes maintain stability while preserving sufficient adaptive capacity.

Characteristics include:

* effective legitimacy production;
* functioning information feedback channels;
* flexible resource allocation;
* capacity for institutional adjustment.

In this condition, stability mechanisms remain primarily adaptive.

The relationship can be expressed as:

[
Stability Mechanisms \rightarrow Resilience \rightarrow Adaptation
]

The regime survives because it maintains both control capacity and learning capacity.

---

## 2. Intensified Governance (S2): The Transition Zone

The second trajectory represents a transitional condition in which regimes increasingly rely on stabilization mechanisms to manage uncertainty.

This stage is theoretically important because it does not represent inevitable decline.

Instead, intensified governance can generate two possible outcomes:

### Path A: Adaptive Recalibration

If institutions retain feedback capacity and adjustment mechanisms, intensified governance may return to adaptive stability.

[
S2 \rightarrow S1
]

### Path B: Rigidity Accumulation

If control mechanisms increasingly suppress feedback, concentrate legitimacy, and reinforce dependency structures, the system may move toward rigidity.

[

]

## 3. Structural Rigidity Crisis (S3): The Loss of Adaptive Capacity

The third trajectory emerges when stabilization mechanisms become increasingly self-reinforcing and reduce the system’s ability to adjust.

Structural rigidity does not necessarily imply immediate regime collapse. Rather, it describes a condition in which:

* policy correction becomes increasingly difficult;
* information distortion increases;
* institutional flexibility declines;
* elite coordination becomes more dependent on existing arrangements.

The defining feature of S3 is not weak state capacity, but a mismatch between **control capacity** and **adaptive capacity**.

A regime may remain highly capable of controlling society while becoming less capable of responding effectively to changing conditions.

---

# Central Proposition of the ARR Model

The ARR Model advances the following theoretical proposition:

> **Authoritarian regimes become vulnerable not necessarily because their stability mechanisms fail, but because those mechanisms may become excessively successful at preserving existing structures while reducing the system’s capacity for adaptation.**

This proposition differs from traditional theories of authoritarian durability in three ways.

---

## First, stability is reconceptualized as a dynamic process

Traditional approaches often treat authoritarian stability as an accumulated outcome of institutional strength.

The ARR Model argues that stability must be analyzed across time:

[
Short-term Stability \neq Long-term Adaptability
]

A mechanism that increases survival today may constrain adjustment tomorrow.

---

## Second, control and adaptation are treated as analytically distinct dimensions

The model rejects the assumption that greater control automatically produces greater resilience.

Instead:

[
Authoritarian Durability =
Control Capacity + Adaptive Capacity
]

A regime may possess high control capacity but declining adaptive capacity.

This distinction explains why some highly controlled systems experience prolonged stagnation or sudden transformation.

---

## Third, authoritarian transformation is conceptualized as nonlinear

The ARR Model does not assume gradual decline.

Instead, it proposes threshold dynamics.

A system may remain stable despite accumulating pressures until several conditions interact:

* legitimacy erosion;
* feedback deterioration;
* elite coordination problems;
* institutional rigidity.

Once critical thresholds are reached, previously manageable pressures may generate rapid transformation.

The process can be represented as:

[
Accumulated Rigidity + Reduced Adaptability
\rightarrow
Threshold Crossing
\rightarrow
Nonlinear Transformation
]

---

# Model Implications

The ARR Model generates three broader theoretical implications for comparative authoritarian research.

---

## Implication 1: Authoritarian stability contains an internal tension

Authoritarian resilience should not be understood as a simple increase in governing strength.

Instead, resilience contains a potential contradiction:

[
More Stabilization
\rightarrow
More Survival Capacity

but under certain conditions:

[
More Stabilization
\rightarrow
Less Adaptability
]

This tension explains why authoritarian systems may appear increasingly stable while simultaneously accumulating structural vulnerabilities.

---

## Implication 2: Digital governance changes the relationship between control and feedback

Digital technologies do not simply increase authoritarian capacity.

They transform the relationship between:

* surveillance;
* information;
* social coordination;
* institutional learning.

The key theoretical question is therefore not whether digital technologies strengthen authoritarian control, but whether they alter the balance between control capacity and adaptive capacity.

---

## Implication 3: Authoritarian trajectories depend on adaptive capacity preservation

The ARR Model predicts that authoritarian regimes with similar levels of control capacity may follow different trajectories depending on whether they preserve institutional feedback and adjustment mechanisms.

Therefore:

High control capacity does not necessarily produce the same outcome.

Instead:

[
Control Capacity + Feedback Capacity
\rightarrow
Adaptive Stability
]

while:

[
Control Capacity - Feedback Capacity
\rightarrow
Rigidity Accumulation
]

---

# Conclusion of the Theory Section

The Authoritarian Resilience-Rigidity Transformation Model provides a dynamic framework for understanding authoritarian durability and transformation. Rather than viewing authoritarian stability and authoritarian crisis as separate phenomena, the model conceptualizes them as different outcomes generated by the same institutional mechanisms operating under different conditions.

The core theoretical contribution is the identification of the **Stability Transformation Effect**: the process through which mechanisms designed to preserve authoritarian stability may gradually transform into sources of institutional rigidity when they undermine adaptive capacity.

This framework provides the theoretical foundation for deriving testable hypotheses regarding legitimacy concentration, digital control, feedback suppression, resource dependency, elite coordination, and nonlinear political transformation.

The following section develops these hypotheses and specifies the observable implications of the ARR Model.

# 3.1 Legitimacy Concentration and Narrative Rigidity:

# The Legitimacy Monoculture Hypothesis

## 3.1 合法性集中与叙事刚性：合法性单一化假设

---

## Introduction

A central question in authoritarian politics concerns how regimes generate and maintain political compliance over time. Classical theories of authoritarian rule often emphasize coercion as a fundamental mechanism of survival. However, comparative research over recent decades has demonstrated that authoritarian durability cannot be adequately explained by repression alone. Many authoritarian regimes actively construct sources of legitimacy, develop institutional narratives, and cultivate forms of political acceptance that reduce reliance on coercive enforcement.

The literature on authoritarian resilience has therefore increasingly examined legitimacy as a core mechanism of regime survival. Authoritarian governments seek not only to prevent opposition but also to establish justificatory frameworks that explain why existing political arrangements should be maintained. These frameworks may derive from economic performance, ideological commitments, institutional effectiveness, national identity, historical narratives, or combinations of these sources.

Existing scholarship has largely treated legitimacy production as a resilience-enhancing mechanism. The underlying assumption is that stronger legitimacy provides authoritarian regimes with greater political stability by reducing uncertainty, strengthening elite cohesion, and lowering the costs of governance. However, this perspective often treats legitimacy as a cumulative resource rather than a dynamic system with potential constraints.

This section introduces a theoretical extension through the **Authoritarian Resilience-Rigidity Transformation Model (ARR Model)**. The central argument is that legitimacy production has a dual effect. While legitimacy mechanisms can enhance authoritarian stability in the short term, excessive concentration of legitimacy sources may create **legitimacy monoculture**, producing narrative rigidity and reducing adaptive capacity over time.

The key theoretical proposition is:

> Authoritarian regimes become vulnerable not necessarily when legitimacy mechanisms fail, but when legitimacy production becomes excessively concentrated and loses the flexibility required for adaptation.

This process is conceptualized as the **Legitimacy Monoculture Mechanism**.

---

# 1. Legitimacy Production and Authoritarian Resilience

## 1.1 Performance Legitimacy: Stability through Output-Based Justification

One influential approach in authoritarian studies emphasizes **performance legitimacy** as a foundation of regime durability. Unlike democratic systems that derive legitimacy primarily from electoral competition, authoritarian regimes may justify their authority through their capacity to deliver economic development, public goods, administrative effectiveness, or social order.

Performance legitimacy operates through an implicit exchange mechanism:

[
Governance Effectiveness
\rightarrow
Public Acceptance
\rightarrow
Political Stability
]

When citizens perceive the state as capable of improving material conditions or maintaining social stability, the regime may reduce reliance on coercive mechanisms.

From the perspective of authoritarian resilience theory, performance legitimacy provides several advantages.

First, it creates a flexible source of justification because it can adapt to changing social expectations. Second, it allows regimes to present political authority as instrumentally valuable rather than merely imposed. Third, it can strengthen elite cohesion by linking political loyalty to developmental outcomes.

Therefore, existing research generally views performance legitimacy as a stabilizing mechanism because it provides regimes with resources for maintaining support and managing uncertainty.

However, performance legitimacy also contains potential vulnerabilities. Because it depends on continuous demonstrations of effectiveness, it may become sensitive to declining performance or changing expectations. A legitimacy system heavily dependent on performance outcomes may face greater adjustment challenges when those outcomes become difficult to sustain.

---

## 1.2 Ideological Legitimacy: Stability through Meaning Construction

A second major source of authoritarian legitimacy is ideological legitimacy.

Ideological legitimacy operates by providing a broader interpretive framework that explains:

* why political authority exists;
* why existing institutions are justified;
* what collective goals society should pursue.

Unlike performance legitimacy, which depends primarily on outcomes, ideological legitimacy provides symbolic coherence and normative justification.

Theoretical research on authoritarian politics has demonstrated that ideological systems can enhance regime durability by creating shared meanings and reducing uncertainty about political order.

The mechanism operates as:

[
Ideological Narrative
\rightarrow
Collective Identity
\rightarrow
Political Cohesion
]

Ideological legitimacy can therefore reduce political competition by shaping how citizens interpret political alternatives.

However, ideological legitimacy also depends on narrative flexibility. A political narrative that can incorporate new circumstances may provide long-term adaptability, whereas a highly rigid ideological framework may constrain policy adjustment and limit the ability to reinterpret changing realities.

Thus, ideology can function either as an adaptive resource or as a source of rigidity depending on its degree of concentration and flexibility.

---

## 1.3 Institutional Legitimacy: Stability through Organizational Capacity

A third approach emphasizes institutional legitimacy.

Institutional legitimacy refers to the extent to which political authority is accepted because governing institutions are perceived as capable, predictable, and effective.

Authoritarian institutions—including ruling organizations, bureaucratic systems, and administrative structures—may generate legitimacy by demonstrating:

* organizational competence;
* policy consistency;
* administrative reliability.

Institutional legitimacy contributes to authoritarian resilience by reducing uncertainty among elites and social actors.

The mechanism can be represented as:

[
Institutional Effectiveness
\rightarrow
Predictability
\rightarrow
Political Stability
]

Existing institutional theories therefore emphasize that authoritarian survival often depends on organizational structures rather than personal rule or coercive domination alone.

However, institutional legitimacy also raises an important theoretical question:

When institutional legitimacy becomes increasingly tied to preserving existing arrangements, can institutional strength become institutional rigidity?

The ARR Model argues that this possibility emerges when legitimacy sources become increasingly concentrated and mutually reinforcing.

---

# 2. From Legitimacy Accumulation to Legitimacy Monoculture

Existing theories of authoritarian resilience generally assume that additional legitimacy resources increase regime stability. According to this perspective:

[
More Legitimacy Sources
\rightarrow
Greater Political Stability
]

The ARR Model introduces a different theoretical perspective.

It argues that the relationship between legitimacy and stability is not linear.

Instead:

[
Legitimacy Production
\rightarrow
Short-term Stability
]

but under conditions of excessive concentration:

[
Legitimacy Concentration
\rightarrow
Narrative Rigidity
\rightarrow
Reduced Adaptability
]

The key distinction is between **legitimacy diversity** and **legitimacy monoculture**.

---

## Legitimacy Diversity

A regime with multiple legitimacy foundations possesses greater flexibility.

For example, political stability may be supported through a combination of:

* performance;
* institutional effectiveness;
* ideological narratives;
* national identity;
* social expectations.

In such systems, weakness in one legitimacy dimension may be compensated by others.

The system has greater adaptive capacity because it possesses alternative justificatory resources.

---

## Legitimacy Monoculture

By contrast, legitimacy monoculture occurs when political authority becomes increasingly dependent on a narrow and concentrated source of justification.

This concentration may take several forms:

1. **Ideological concentration**

A single interpretive framework dominates political justification.

2. **Leader-centered legitimacy**

Political authority becomes increasingly associated with a specific leadership figure or symbolic representation.

3. **Narrative dependence**

The regime relies heavily on maintaining consistency within a particular political narrative.

The short-term effect of legitimacy monoculture can be positive.

It creates:

* greater ideological coherence;
* stronger elite alignment;
* clearer political messaging;
* reduced uncertainty.

However, the long-term effect may be negative because concentrated legitimacy systems have fewer mechanisms for adjustment.

When external conditions change, the regime faces a dilemma:

Maintaining narrative consistency preserves short-term coherence but limits adaptation.

Changing the narrative preserves adaptability but risks undermining previously constructed legitimacy.

This tension constitutes the central mechanism of narrative rigidity.

---

# 3. The Causal Mechanism: From Legitimacy Concentration to Adaptive Decline

The ARR Model proposes the following causal sequence:

[
\textbf{Legitimacy Concentration}
]

↓

[
\textbf{Narrative Flexibility Decline}
]

↓

[
\textbf{Adaptive Capacity Reduction}
]

↓

[
\textbf{Higher Vulnerability to Legitimacy Shocks}
]

---

## Stage 1: Legitimacy Concentration

As legitimacy becomes concentrated, political systems increasingly depend on maintaining consistency within limited justificatory frameworks.

This reduces the availability of alternative legitimacy strategies.

---

## Stage 2: Narrative Flexibility Decline

Highly concentrated legitimacy systems experience reduced narrative flexibility.

The problem is not the existence of ideology or political narratives themselves.

Rather, the issue is the inability to revise narratives without threatening political coherence.

Therefore:

[
Narrative Centralization
\rightarrow
Narrative Rigidity
]

---

## Stage 3: Adaptive Capacity Reduction

Reduced narrative flexibility affects institutional adaptation.

Political systems require the ability to reinterpret challenges, revise policies, and adjust expectations.

When legitimacy narratives become rigid, policy adjustment may become politically costly.

Thus:

[
Narrative Rigidity
\rightarrow
Adaptation Constraint
]

---

## Stage 4: Increased Vulnerability to Legitimacy Shocks

A legitimacy shock occurs when existing justification mechanisms become insufficient to explain changing circumstances.

Examples of such shocks include:

* declining performance;
* unexpected social changes;
* institutional failures;
* external disruptions.

The vulnerability does not result simply from the existence of problems.

Rather, vulnerability emerges because the legitimacy system lacks flexibility to absorb those problems.

---

# 4. Hypothesis Development

Based on the preceding theoretical argument, this article proposes the following hypothesis:

> **The greater the concentration of authoritarian legitimacy sources, the higher the short-term stability but the lower the regime’s capacity to recover from legitimacy shocks.**

This hypothesis contains two related predictions.

First, legitimacy concentration should increase short-term stability because concentrated narratives reduce uncertainty, strengthen political coordination, and improve message consistency.

Second, legitimacy concentration should decrease long-term adaptive capacity because it reduces narrative flexibility and limits alternative strategies of legitimacy reconstruction.

Therefore, the relationship between legitimacy concentration and authoritarian resilience is expected to be temporally conditional:

The Legitimacy Monoculture Hypothesis extends existing theories of authoritarian resilience by introducing a temporal dimension to legitimacy production. # 3.2 Digital Control and Feedback Suppression:

The emergence of digital technologies has fundamentally transformed the mechanisms through which authoritarian regimes govern, monitor, and interact with society. Earlier theories of authoritarian stability primarily focused on traditional instruments of political control, including coercive institutions, censorship systems, bureaucratic organizations, and surveillance networks. However, the expansion of digital technologies has introduced new forms of governance capacity that significantly alter the relationship between state authority, information flows, and social organization.

The literature on **digital authoritarianism** has demonstrated that contemporary authoritarian regimes increasingly utilize digital infrastructures to enhance political control. Through digital surveillance, information regulation, and algorithmic governance, states can monitor social behavior, identify potential sources of instability, and intervene more efficiently in collective action processes.

Existing scholarship has primarily emphasized the stabilizing effects of digital governance. From this perspective, digital technologies increase authoritarian resilience by expanding the state’s capacity to collect information, regulate communication, and prevent opposition coordination.

\rightarrow

\rightarrow
Threat Detection
\rightarrow
Political Stability

# 3.3 Resource Binding and Political Exit:

# The Exit without Voice Hypothesis

## 3.3 资源绑定与政治退出：无声退出假设

---

## Introduction

A central puzzle in authoritarian politics concerns why some regimes maintain social stability despite the existence of latent dissatisfaction or declining political engagement. Traditional theories of authoritarian durability have emphasized coercive capacity, institutional adaptation, and legitimacy construction as explanations for regime survival. However, authoritarian stability is also produced through less visible mechanisms: the organization of dependence between political authority and access to resources.

Authoritarian regimes frequently maintain stability not only by restricting political alternatives but also by structuring economic and social opportunities. Through control over employment, career advancement, economic privileges, and institutional access, regimes can shape individual incentives and alter the strategic choices available to social actors.

This mechanism represents a form of **resource binding**.

The conventional interpretation of resource binding emphasizes its stabilizing function. When citizens, elites, and organizations depend on state-controlled resources, the costs of political confrontation increase, while incentives for cooperation become stronger. From this perspective, resource dependence contributes to authoritarian resilience by reducing opposition incentives and strengthening political incorporation.

However, the **Authoritarian Resilience-Rigidity Transformation Model (ARR Model)** introduces a theoretical extension.

The model argues that resource binding has a dual effect.

In the short term:

[
Resource Dependency
\rightarrow
Compliance
\rightarrow
Political Stability
]

However, over time:

[
Resource Dependency
\rightarrow
Reduced Voice Capacity
\rightarrow
Private Exit Behavior
]

The central argument is that resource dependence does not necessarily eliminate dissatisfaction. Instead, it may transform the behavioral expression of dissatisfaction.

Rather than producing collective political opposition, dissatisfaction may be redirected toward individualized forms of withdrawal.

This article conceptualizes this process as the **Exit without Voice Mechanism**.

The core theoretical proposition is:

> A decline in visible political opposition does not necessarily indicate increased political support; under conditions of high resource dependency, dissatisfaction may shift from collective voice toward private exit.

By extending Albert Hirschman’s classic framework of **Exit, Voice, and Loyalty**, the ARR Model demonstrates how authoritarian institutions reshape the relationship between dissatisfaction, political expression, and social withdrawal.

---

# 1. Resource Binding and Authoritarian Stability

## 1.1 Economic Dependence as a Mechanism of Political Control

Existing research on authoritarian resilience has demonstrated that regimes often maintain stability through the strategic management of economic relationships.

Unlike democratic systems where access to economic opportunities may be relatively independent from political authority, authoritarian systems frequently possess greater capacity to influence the distribution of valuable resources.

These resources may include:

* employment opportunities;
* access to regulated markets;
* welfare benefits;
* professional opportunities;
* economic privileges.

Through these mechanisms, political authorities create networks of dependence between individuals and institutions.

The underlying logic is:

[
State-Controlled Resources
\rightarrow
Dependency Formation
\rightarrow
Political Compliance
]

Economic dependence affects political behavior by changing the cost-benefit calculation of opposition.

When individuals rely heavily on state-controlled resources, political confrontation becomes increasingly costly.

The consequence is not necessarily ideological agreement.

Rather, individuals may choose political restraint because alternative strategies appear risky.

Thus, resource binding generates stability through a combination of incentives and constraints.

---

## 1.2 Career Incentives and Elite Incorporation

A second dimension of resource binding concerns career incentives.

Authoritarian regimes often maintain elite stability through systems of political and professional advancement.

Career opportunities may depend on:

* bureaucratic membership;
* institutional loyalty;
* organizational affiliation;
* access to political networks.

These mechanisms serve two functions.

First, they reduce incentives for elite defection by linking personal advancement to regime continuity.

Second, they integrate potential challengers into existing institutional structures.

The mechanism can be expressed as:

[
Career Incentives
\rightarrow
Elite Dependence
\rightarrow
Reduced Opposition Incentives
]

# 3.6 Information Distortion and Adaptive Decline:

# Introduction

A fundamental challenge in authoritarian governance concerns the relationship between political control and state learning capacity. Existing research has extensively examined how authoritarian regimes manage information environments in order to maintain political stability. Through censorship, media regulation, and communication control, authoritarian governments can reduce the visibility of opposition, constrain collective mobilization, and shape public discourse.

From this perspective, information control represents an important instrument of authoritarian resilience.

The conventional theoretical expectation is:

[

\rightarrow
Reduced Opposition Visibility
\rightarrow
Political Stability
]

However, the **Authoritarian Resilience-Rigidity Transformation Model (ARR Model)** introduces a theoretical extension.

It argues that information control has a dual effect.

While information control can enhance short-term regime stability by limiting disruptive information flows within society, it may simultaneously alter the internal information environment of the state itself.

The central theoretical argument is:

> The same mechanisms that reduce politically threatening information may also reduce the availability of accurate information required for effective governance.

Therefore, authoritarian information control creates a potential paradox:

[
Control Capacity \uparrow
]

while:

[
Adaptive Capacity \downarrow
]

This section develops the **Information Distortion Hypothesis**, which argues that highly controlled information systems may generate systematic distortions that weaken institutional learning and policy adaptation.

[

]

[

]

The theoretical contribution is to connect two previously separated research traditions:

* authoritarian control theory;
* state capacity and governance learning theory.

The central proposition is:

> Political systems may possess high capacity to control information without possessing equivalent capacity to process information accurately.

---

# 1. Existing Theories of Information Control and Authoritarian Governance

## 1.1 Authoritarian Information Control: Stability through Communication Management

A significant body of comparative authoritarian research has emphasized information control as a central mechanism of regime survival.

Unlike democratic systems where information competition and institutional accountability create relatively open communication environments, authoritarian regimes often seek to manage the circulation, visibility, and interpretation of information.

Information control mechanisms include:

* censorship;
* media regulation;
* narrative management;
* communication restriction.

The primary theoretical assumption is that information control strengthens authoritarian resilience by reducing opportunities for opposition coordination.

The mechanism is:

[
Information Restriction
\rightarrow
Reduced Mobilization Capacity
\rightarrow
Lower Political Threat
]

From this perspective, controlling information serves several stabilizing functions.

First, it prevents rapid diffusion of oppositional narratives.

Second, it reduces coordination opportunities among potential challengers.

Third, it allows political authorities to shape public interpretations of events.

Therefore, existing theories have generally treated information control as a mechanism of political stabilization.

However, this literature has primarily focused on the effects of information control on society.

The ARR Model introduces a different analytical question:

> How does information control affect the information available to the state itself?

---

# 1.2 Bureaucratic Information Filtering: The Internal Politics of Information Transmission

Beyond direct censorship, authoritarian systems also face internal information problems.

Information generated within society must pass through bureaucratic institutions before reaching central decision-makers.

Bureaucratic actors may have incentives to:

The mechanism is:

[
Social Information
\rightarrow
Bureaucratic Processing
\rightarrow
Political Transmission

Existing bureaucratic theories demonstrate that information transmission is rarely neutral.

Organizations interpret and select information according to institutional incentives.

In authoritarian contexts, these incentives may be intensified because negative information can create political risks.

Therefore, bureaucratic actors may engage in strategic presentation of information.

The consequence is:

[
Observed Reality
\neq
Actual Conditions
]

However, traditional studies often treat bureaucratic filtering as an organizational problem rather than a systemic consequence of information control.

The ARR Model argues that when information control becomes institutionalized, filtering mechanisms may become embedded within the entire governance system.

# 1.3 Political Accountability Problems and Governance Learning

A third relevant literature concerns political accountability.

[

\rightarrow

\rightarrow

This creates a distinction between:

## Control-oriented information systems

A system may be highly effective at controlling information flows while becoming less effective at using information for adaptation.

# 3.7 Intensified Governance and Divergent Trajectories:

# The Intensification Dual Path Hypothesis

## 3.7 强化治理与分化路径：强化治理双路径假设

The previous sections have demonstrated that authoritarian stability mechanisms may generate both resilience and rigidity. However, an important theoretical question remains unresolved: **under what conditions does authoritarian strengthening enhance regime durability, and under what conditions does it contribute to long-term rigidity?**

Existing theories of authoritarian adaptation have extensively examined how regimes respond to threats through institutional innovation, organizational restructuring, and expanded governance capacity. Yet these approaches often conceptualize intensified governance as a relatively unified phenomenon: when authoritarian regimes face rising uncertainty, they increase control, strengthen institutions, and improve their capacity to maintain order.

The Authoritarian Resilience-Rigidity Transformation Model (ARR Model) introduces a different perspective. It argues that intensified governance does not represent a single trajectory. Instead, it constitutes a critical transitional state in which authoritarian systems may follow divergent paths depending on whether they preserve or undermine adaptive capacity.

Accordingly, this article proposes the **Intensification Dual Path Hypothesis**:

> Authoritarian intensification can either reinforce adaptive stability or accelerate rigidity accumulation, depending on whether expanded governance capacity is accompanied by preserved feedback mechanisms and institutional adjustment capabilities.

This argument challenges the assumption that stronger governance necessarily produces either greater resilience or greater vulnerability. Instead, it conceptualizes intensified governance as a conditional process whose consequences depend on the interaction between control expansion and adaptive capacity.

---

# 3.7.1 Existing Theories of Authoritarian Adaptation and Governance Intensification

## Authoritarian Adaptation Theory

Research on authoritarian adaptation has fundamentally changed the understanding of authoritarian durability. Rather than viewing authoritarian regimes as static systems dependent primarily on coercion, this literature emphasizes their ability to adjust institutions, modify strategies, and respond to emerging challenges.

The central insight of authoritarian adaptation theory is that survival depends on continuous adjustment.

Authoritarian regimes adapt through mechanisms such as:

* institutional redesign;
* elite management;
* bureaucratic innovation;
* policy experimentation;
* selective responsiveness.

Within this framework, governance strengthening is generally interpreted as a rational response to uncertainty.

The underlying logic is:

[
External/Internal Threat Increase
\rightarrow
Institutional Adaptation
\rightarrow
Enhanced Stability
]

This perspective has provided important explanations for authoritarian persistence. However, it often treats adaptation capacity and control capacity as mutually reinforcing.

The ARR Model challenges this assumption by arguing that adaptation and control may diverge.

A regime may increase its capacity to control political uncertainty while simultaneously reducing its capacity to process information and adjust institutions.

Therefore:

[
Control Capacity \neq Adaptive Capacity
]

The distinction between these two forms of capacity is essential for explaining divergent authoritarian trajectories.

---

# Authoritarian Upgrading

A second relevant theoretical tradition examines **authoritarian upgrading**.

This literature argues that contemporary authoritarian regimes have developed sophisticated governance techniques, including:

* institutionalized ruling structures;
* professionalized bureaucracies;
* advanced surveillance systems;
* legal and administrative instruments.

\rightarrow
Regime Durability
]

However, the ARR Model identifies a theoretical limitation.

Authoritarian upgrading research primarily explains **how regimes become more capable**, but less frequently examines whether increasing capability may generate internal constraints.

Specifically, it does not fully address whether:

* stronger monitoring reduces information diversity;
* stronger institutional control reduces flexibility;
* stronger coordination reduces autonomous adjustment.

Therefore, the ARR Model extends authoritarian upgrading theory by introducing a temporal distinction:

Short-term upgrading may enhance resilience.

Long-term over-institutionalization may generate rigidity.

---

This perspective examines periods when regimes respond to uncertainty by expanding:

* surveillance;
* censorship;
* administrative restrictions;

[

[

If tightening suppresses feedback:

[
Control + Feedback Suppression
\rightarrow
Rigidity Accumulation

> Authoritarian regimes do not move toward crisis simply because they intensify governance. They become vulnerable when intensified governance disrupts the feedback, flexibility, and adaptive mechanisms necessary for long-term institutional adjustment.

This perspective allows comparative analysis of why some authoritarian systems successfully recalibrate after periods of intensified governance, while others enter prolonged rigidity and structural vulnerability.

# 3.8 Sub-stable Authoritarian Equilibrium:

# The Institutional Freeze Hypothesis

## 3.8 亚稳定威权均衡：制度冻结假设

A central challenge in comparative authoritarian studies is explaining why some authoritarian regimes experience neither rapid transformation nor institutional collapse, despite accumulating structural constraints. While existing theories have extensively examined authoritarian durability and authoritarian breakdown, they have paid comparatively less attention to a third trajectory: **long-term political persistence accompanied by declining adaptive capacity**.

The Authoritarian Resilience-Rigidity Transformation Model (ARR Model) introduces the concept of **Sub-stable Authoritarian Equilibrium** to explain this overlooked condition.

Sub-stable authoritarian equilibrium refers to a political condition in which authoritarian systems maintain prolonged stability through effective control mechanisms while simultaneously experiencing limited institutional adaptation.

Unlike stable adaptive regimes, these systems do not continuously adjust to changing environments. Unlike collapsing regimes, they retain sufficient organizational and coercive capacity to prevent immediate breakdown.

They represent a distinct political trajectory:

[
High\ Control\ Capacity
+
Low\ Adaptive\ Capacity
\rightarrow
Stable\ but\ Rigid\ Equilibrium
]

The central argument of this section is that authoritarian persistence does not always indicate institutional strength. Some regimes survive not because they maintain high levels of adaptation, but because they achieve a relatively stable equilibrium between effective control and constrained adjustment.

This condition is defined as:

> **A political equilibrium characterized by prolonged regime durability, limited institutional innovation, restricted feedback mechanisms, and reduced adaptive flexibility.**

The Institutional Freeze Hypothesis explains why certain authoritarian systems may remain stable for extended periods while gradually losing the capacity for meaningful institutional transformation.

---

Much of the comparative authoritarian literature has historically organized political trajectories around two primary outcomes:

2. authoritarian breakdown.

* institutional adaptation;
* elite coordination;
* legitimacy management;
* organizational capacity.

* legitimacy crises;
* economic shocks;
* mass mobilization;

# Theoretical Limitations of Existing Approaches

## Authoritarian Resilience Theory

Authoritarian resilience theory has successfully demonstrated that authoritarian regimes can survive through institutional innovation.

However, its emphasis on adaptive capacity creates a theoretical limitation.

If resilience is primarily understood as the ability to adjust, then systems that survive through rigidity become difficult to explain.

The key question becomes:

> Can authoritarian survival occur through reduced adaptation rather than increased adaptation?

The ARR Model answers this question affirmatively.

* control mechanisms remain effective;

However, these theories frequently overlook the possibility that authoritarian systems can remain stable even while accumulating rigidity.

The absence of collapse is not necessarily evidence of successful adaptation.

## Historical Institutionalism and Institutional Persistence

The ARR Model extends this perspective by introducing the concept of:

## Institutional Freeze

Institutional freeze occurs when existing arrangements become sufficiently effective at preserving themselves that meaningful adaptation becomes increasingly difficult.

The system remains stable because change becomes costly.

# 3.9 Legitimacy Diversity and Adaptive Resilience:

# The Legitimacy Diversity Hypothesis

## 3.9 合法性多元化与适应性韧性：合法性多元化假设

A central question in comparative authoritarian studies concerns how regimes maintain political support under changing social, economic, and international conditions. Existing legitimacy theories have demonstrated that authoritarian regimes do not rely exclusively on coercion but often construct multiple sources of political acceptance, including economic performance, ideological narratives, institutional arrangements, and national identity.

However, existing research has primarily examined **the strength of legitimacy** rather than **the structure of legitimacy**.

The conventional assumption is:

[
Higher\ Legitimacy
\rightarrow
Greater\ Stability
]

The Authoritarian Resilience-Rigidity Transformation Model (ARR Model) introduces a different analytical perspective.

It argues that the resilience effects of legitimacy depend not only on how much legitimacy a regime possesses, but also on how legitimacy is structured.

Specifically, this article distinguishes between:

1. **Legitimacy Monoculture**
2. **Legitimacy Diversity**

A legitimacy monoculture occurs when authoritarian regimes become highly dependent on a single source of political justification. Such concentration may increase short-term coherence but reduce adaptive flexibility when that source faces disruption.

By contrast, legitimacy diversity refers to the coexistence of multiple legitimacy foundations that allow regimes to adjust narratives and policy priorities in response to changing environments.

The central argument is:

> Legitimacy diversity enhances authoritarian adaptive resilience by reducing dependence on any single legitimacy source and increasing narrative flexibility during periods of uncertainty.

Therefore, the ARR Model does not argue that legitimacy accumulation inevitably produces rigidity. Instead, it proposes that **the organizational structure of legitimacy determines whether legitimacy functions as a source of resilience or vulnerability.**

# 3.10 State Capacity and Authoritarian Trajectories:

# 4.4 Measuring Authoritarian Resilience:

# The Authoritarian Resilience Index (ARI)

## 4.4 衡量威权韧性：威权韧性指数（Authoritarian Resilience Index）

A central objective of the Authoritarian Resilience-Rigidity Transformation Model (ARR Model) is to move beyond binary conceptualizations of authoritarian outcomes.

Existing comparative authoritarian research has generated substantial knowledge regarding why authoritarian regimes survive, yet the measurement of authoritarian resilience remains theoretically fragmented.

Many existing indicators focus primarily on:

* regime duration;
* repression capacity;
* institutional survival;
* elite stability.

Although these dimensions capture important aspects of authoritarian durability, they do not fully distinguish between different forms of stability.

The ARR Model argues that authoritarian resilience is not equivalent to regime survival.

A regime may survive through:

1. adaptive institutional capacity;

or through:

2. accumulated control and reduced flexibility.

Therefore, the central measurement problem is:

> How can researchers distinguish adaptive resilience from rigid stability?

To address this problem, this article develops the **Authoritarian Resilience Index (ARI)**.

The ARI is designed as a multidimensional measurement framework that captures the institutional foundations enabling authoritarian systems to maintain stability while preserving adaptive capacity.

The theoretical assumption is:

[
Authoritarian\ Resilience
\neq
Political\ Survival
]

Instead:

[
Authoritarian\ Resilience
=========================

Institutional\ Coordination
+
Legitimacy\ Capacity
+
Governance\ Capacity
]

The index operationalizes three core dimensions:

1. Institutional Coordination;
2. Legitimacy Capacity;
3. Governance Capacity.

These dimensions correspond directly to the causal mechanisms identified in the ARR Model.

---

# 4.4.1 Theoretical Definition of the Authoritarian Resilience Index

## ARI的理论定义

The Authoritarian Resilience Index is defined as:

> **A multidimensional measure of an authoritarian regime’s capacity to maintain political stability through institutional coordination, legitimacy maintenance, and effective governance while preserving adaptive potential.**

This definition contains three theoretical components.

---

## First, resilience is institutional rather than merely coercive

Traditional approaches often associate authoritarian durability with:

* repression;
* surveillance;
* coercive organizations.

However, the ARR Model argues that coercion represents only one dimension of stability.

Long-term resilience depends on whether political systems can coordinate actors, maintain legitimacy, and implement policies.

Therefore:

[
Coercion
\neq
Resilience
]

Instead:

[
Coercion
+
Coordination
+
Adaptation
==========

Resilience
]

---

## Second, resilience is multidimensional

Authoritarian systems maintain stability through different mechanisms.

A regime may possess:

* strong elite coordination but weak legitimacy;
* strong legitimacy but weak governance;
* strong governance but limited institutional flexibility.

Therefore, a single indicator cannot capture authoritarian resilience.

The ARI adopts a multidimensional approach.

---

## Third, resilience includes adaptive capacity

The ARR Model distinguishes between:

## Adaptive resilience

and:

## Rigid stability.

A high ARI score should represent not simply the ability to survive pressure but the ability to adjust without losing institutional coherence.

Therefore, the ARI measures the foundations that allow stability mechanisms to remain adaptive.

---
